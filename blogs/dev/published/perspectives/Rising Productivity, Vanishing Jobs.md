# Rising Productivity and the End of Workers As We Know Them

There's one thing I just can't shake lately when I'm working. It's AI.

AI has been a huge benefit to me — work, studying, you name it. So much has gotten easier. I think of myself as a developer who's lived through both sides: the world before AI and the world after it. It genuinely feels like I'm standing right in the middle of that transition.

A lot of smart people keep saying the same thing:

> "AI is going to massively boost productivity."

And honestly, I agree. At this point, it's really just a question of **when you get on board** — not whether you should. Why ride a horse and buggy when cars already exist?

---

## Everyday Life Has Already Changed

This isn't some far-off hypothetical scenario. AI is already baked into our daily lives. Here's a small example from mine.

There was a product on Starbucks' online store that I really wanted, but it was always sold out. At first, I kept manually checking the site. But after a while, I realized I was wasting a ridiculous amount of time doing that.

So I used AI to quickly build an automation bot:

- Checks the stock of that specific Starbucks item every 4 hours
- Runs automatically on a GitHub Actions cron job
- Shoots me a Gmail alert when it's back in stock

A year or two ago, I probably would've thought, "It's faster to just check it myself." But with AI, I knocked the whole thing out in a few prompts.

That's a *tiny* example. But the bigger point is this: **the effort required to increase productivity is getting smaller and smaller.** And this trend is only going to speed up — spreading into every industry.

---

## How Is the Developer Role Changing?

When you look at this shift through a developer lens, some really interesting patterns show up.

Frontend, backend, infrastructure — those used to be pretty well-defined lanes. Not anymore. The lines are blurring fast. Go a step further and you'll see people handling product planning on top of all that. It's already happening.

Work that used to require a full team can now be handled by just 1 to 3 developers. If companies don't cut headcount, developers are naturally going to start picking up responsibilities way outside their original job description.

And when a smaller crew is juggling different roles, the way people work changes too. More remote work, more async communication, and more collaboration across completely different contexts. The more that happens, the more **people who can actually communicate well** become invaluable.

You might be thinking, "Doesn't AI translate everything anyway? Language won't matter."

But we're not quite there yet. In my view, if two candidates have the same skill set, the one who actually speaks the language is going to win out every time. At the end of the day, work is still a people game.

---

## So Who's Going to Make It?

All of this naturally leads to one big question: what kind of person does the AI era actually need?

I think the definition of "competent" is quietly shifting. Raw coding ability still matters — obviously. But looking ahead, **attitude** and **how you think** are probably going to matter more.

I'd break it into two things.

### Personality — "Who Do You Actually Want to Work With?"

As AI takes over more tasks, work is going to split into two buckets:

- **Tasks where it doesn't matter who does them**
- **Tasks where who you're working with makes all the difference**

For that second bucket, it all comes down to the person.

Someone who naturally fits into a team. Someone who shows up consistently. Someone who speaks their mind but knows how to do it without burning bridges. These very human qualities are going to become a serious competitive edge.

### Ability — "Can You Think Ahead?"

Technical chops are table stakes. But the ability I think matters most going forward is **thinking about what comes next**.

Picture the person who says something like:

> "If we go with A, it'll probably lead to B. But wasn't our goal a year from now D? Shouldn't we be doing C instead?"

Not just heads-down on the current ticket — but thinking about where that ticket leads. That kind of forward-thinking is going to become more and more critical.

---

## How I'm Preparing

So what am I actually doing about all this?

I'm Korean, but I work as a developer in Japan, and I've brought my Japanese up to a business-professional level. Now I'm steadily working on English. To me, English isn't just another line on a resume — it's a tool that plugs me into a much bigger world. Maybe it'll extend my career a little longer, too.

(I'll be honest — there's definitely some personal desire mixed in there.)

Working as a developer while operating in two foreign languages at a professional level — I think that says something beyond just language skills. It shows a willingness to keep learning and keep pushing your own boundaries.

*That person never stops learning. They're always getting ready for what's next.* — That's how I'd like to come across.

But the real point isn't the languages themselves. It's **the discipline of constant effort and self-improvement.** I believe that kind of drive can translate to anything — not just software development.

---

## The World Is Already Moving

This isn't just a developer story. Across every profession, productivity is climbing and the effort required is dropping.

If people end up working less — if 4-day or even 3-day workweeks become the norm — what happens? The cost of goods will probably come down. But the cost of human labor? That could actually go *up*.

Think about it: the price of a factory-made cream bun might barely budge, but the wages of the person making it could easily double.

---

Maybe none of this is news to you. Maybe you've heard it all before.

But there's a huge difference between *knowing* something and actually *doing something about it*.

I think we need to keep grinding, keep learning, and keep moving with the current — not against it.

**For the sake of a life we can feel secure in.**
